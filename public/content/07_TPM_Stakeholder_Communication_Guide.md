# 1. Stakeholder Mapping

## Framework

### Why Stakeholder Mapping Is the TPM's First Job

Every program exists within a web of relationships. The TPM who understands this web-who has power, who cares about what, who influences whom-can navigate it effectively. The TPM who doesn't will be constantly surprised by decisions made without their input, support withdrawn unexpectedly, and initiatives blocked by stakeholders they didn't know they needed to engage.

Stakeholder mapping is the systematic process of identifying everyone who has a stake in your program, understanding their interests and influence, and designing an engagement strategy for each one. It's not a one-time exercise. It's a practice you revisit as the program evolves and the stakeholder landscape shifts.

### Research Foundation

Stakeholder mapping draws on stakeholder theory (Freeman, 1984), which established that organizations must manage relationships with all groups that affect or are affected by their activities. In program management, this translates to a structured approach to identifying and engaging stakeholders based on their power, interest, and influence. The Power-Interest Grid (Aubrey Mendelow, 1991) remains the most widely used framework because it balances simplicity with analytical insight-it's easy to apply and reveals meaningful patterns.

### The Stakeholder Mapping Framework (6-Step Method)

#### Step 1: Identify Every Stakeholder

Cast a wide net. Don't filter yet. Consider:

| Category | Who Might Be a Stakeholder |
|----------|---------------------------|
| **Decision-makers** | Program sponsor, steering committee members, VPs, C-suite |
| **Doers** | Engineering leads, architects, product managers, design leads, QA leads |
| **Dependent teams** | Platform teams, security, compliance, legal, data, operations |
| **Customers** | Internal users, external clients, partner organizations |
| **Influencers** | Subject matter experts, senior ICs, informal leaders |
| **External** | Vendors, regulators, industry partners |

**Example - A payment system migration program:**

| Stakeholder | Role | Why They Matter |
|-------------|------|-----------------|
| VP of Engineering | Decision-maker | Approves architecture, allocates engineering resources |
| VP of Product | Decision-maker | Controls feature prioritization, customer commitments |
| Chief Compliance Officer | Decision-maker | Must approve any changes to payment processing |
| Backend Engineering Lead | Doer | Leads the technical implementation |
| Platform Team Lead | Dependent | Their infrastructure must support the new system |
| External Payment Processor | External | Provides the payment API we're integrating with |

#### Step 2: Map Stakeholders on Power × Interest

The Power-Interest Grid categorizes stakeholders by two dimensions:

| | **High Power** | **Low Power** |
|--|--------------|---------------|
| **High Interest** | **Key Players** - Manage closely. Engage frequently. Involve in decisions. | **Keep Informed** - Engage regularly. Ensure they have information. Watch for rising influence. |
| **Low Interest** | **Keep Satisfied** - Monitor. Check in periodically. Anticipate needs. | **Monitor** - Minimal effort. Check periodically for changes. |

**Mapping the payment migration stakeholders:**

| Stakeholder | Power | Interest | Category | Engagement Strategy |
|-------------|-------|----------|----------|-------------------|
| VP of Engineering | High | High | Key Player | Weekly 1:1, involved in architecture decisions |
| VP of Product | High | Medium | Keep Satisfied | Monthly update, involved in timeline decisions |
| Chief Compliance Officer | High | Low | Keep Satisfied (for now) | Quarterly update unless compliance concern arises |
| Backend Engineering Lead | Medium | High | Keep Informed → Key Player | Daily standup, involved in technical decisions |
| Platform Team Lead | Medium | Medium | Keep Informed | Weekly cross-team sync |
| External Payment Processor | Medium | High | Key Player (external) | Weekly integration sync, vendor management |

#### Step 3: Understand Each Stakeholder's Position

For each Key Player and Keep Satisfied stakeholder, answer:

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| **What are their explicit priorities?** | What they've told you matters |
| **What are their implicit concerns?** | What they worry about but may not say (job security, reputation, workload, control) |
| **What pressures are they under?** | What their manager/board/customers are demanding |
| **What is their decision style?** | Data-driven, relationship-driven, authority-driven, consensus-driven |
| **What is their current stance on your program?** | Champion, supporter, neutral, skeptical, blocker |
| **What do you need from them?** | Decisions, approvals, resources, advocacy, information |
| **What do they need from you?** | Visibility, risk mitigation, support for their initiatives |

#### Step 4: Design Your Engagement Strategy

| Category | Engagement Frequency | Engagement Method | Communication Focus |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Key Player** | Weekly | 1:1 meeting or detailed email | Decisions, risks, blockers, progress |
| **Keep Satisfied** | Bi-weekly to monthly | Brief email or scheduled check-in | Status, ask for input, anticipate needs |
| **Keep Informed** | Monthly or per milestone | Group email or newsletter | Progress, upcoming changes, decisions made |
| **Monitor** | Quarterly | Brief check-in | Any changes in their status or interest |

#### Step 5: Build a Stakeholder Engagement Plan

Document your strategy for each Key Player:

**Example - VP of Engineering:**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Stakeholder | Priya, VP of Engineering |
| Category | Key Player |
| Interests | On-time delivery, technical quality, team health, cost efficiency |
| Concerns | Resource contention with other programs, architecture risk |
| Decision style | Data-driven, wants options not just requests |
| What I need | Resource decisions, architecture sign-off, escalation support |
| What they need | Confidence in delivery, early warning of problems, minimized surprises |
| Engagement | Weekly 15-min 1:1 (email if she can't meet), monthly longer review |
| Current stance | Supporter but cautious (past programs have struggled) |

#### Step 6: Review and Update Regularly

Stakeholder landscapes shift. Revisit your map:
- **Monthly:** Check for changes in existing stakeholders' positions
- **Quarterly:** Re-scan for new stakeholders
- **At major milestones:** Stakeholder interests may shift as the program progresses

### Why This Approach Works

The Power-Interest Grid works because it:
1. **Forces prioritization.** Not all stakeholders deserve equal attention. The grid tells you where to invest.
2. **Identifies risks early.** A "Keep Satisfied" stakeholder with low interest who suddenly becomes high interest is a risk you can anticipate.
3. **Prevents neglect.** The grid reminds you of stakeholders you might otherwise ignore until they become a problem.
4. **Creates an action plan.** Each quadrant has a clear engagement strategy. You know what to do.

### Realistic Examples

#### Example 1: The Quietly Blocking Executive

**Situation:** You're a TPM on a 9-month data platform modernization program. The program has been running for 3 months. The VP of Data Engineering, Marcus, has been quiet in steering committee meetings-he doesn't object, but he doesn't actively support either. You marked him as "Keep Satisfied" (high power, medium interest).

**The problem:** In Month 4, Marcus objects to the program's data architecture choices during a steering committee meeting. The objection stalls the program for 2 weeks while a working group evaluates alternatives.

**What went wrong:** Your stakeholder map was incomplete. Marcus had high interest but was expressing it through silence and reserved judgment, not active engagement. You categorized him as "Keep Satisfied" when he should have been "Key Player."

**The fix:** You update your stakeholder map:
- Marcus's real concern: the architecture choice would require his team to restructure their data pipelines, creating 6 months of unplanned work for them
- His decision style: wants to be consulted before decisions, not informed after
- New engagement strategy: bi-weekly 15-minute check-ins to surface concerns before they become objections

**Outcome:** Marcus's concern is addressed in the next architecture review. The working group finds a compromise that works for both programs. Marcus becomes a program supporter.

#### Example 2: The Influential Skeptic

**Situation:** You're leading a program to unify two legacy CRM systems into a single platform. The senior software engineer, Elena, is highly respected by the engineering team but openly skeptical about the program. "We tried this 3 years ago. It failed. What's different this time?"

**Your initial instinct:** Ignore her. She's not a decision-maker. Focus on the VPs.

**The stakeholder mapping insight:** Elena has **low formal power** (she's an IC) but **high informal influence** (the team trusts her judgment). She's on the "Keep Informed" quadrant, but her influence makes her more like a "Key Player."

**Your engagement strategy:**
- You schedule a 30-minute coffee chat. You don't try to convince her. You ask: "What went wrong last time? What would need to be different for this to succeed?"
- Elena shares 3 specific failure points from the previous attempt. You realize 2 of them are still risks in the current plan.
- You address those risks publicly and credit Elena for surfacing them.

**Outcome:** Elena becomes an unofficial advisor. She's still skeptical-but constructively so. When other engineers express doubts, she says: "Actually, they've addressed the main issues from last time. I'm cautiously optimistic." Her endorsement carries more weight than any VP's mandate.

#### Example 3: The Overlooked Dependent Team

**Situation:** You're building a new customer-facing API. Your stakeholder map includes the engineering teams, product, and QA. It doesn't include the **documentation team** because they don't affect your delivery-they just write docs after you ship.

**The problem:** Two weeks before launch, the documentation team says: "We need 4 weeks to write API docs, and your API has changed 3 times in the last month. We can't document what keeps moving."

**What went wrong:** The documentation team was a stakeholder with medium interest (they need to know what's changing) and low formal power (they can't stop the launch). But when they said they couldn't deliver on the original timeline, they effectively became a blocker. They should have been "Keep Informed" with weekly updates on API changes.

**The fix:** You negotiate a compressed documentation timeline (3 weeks instead of 4), commit to API stability for the remaining 3 weeks, and update your stakeholder map to include the documentation team with weekly engagement.

### Case Study

**Organization:** A health insurance company ("WellCare") was building a new member portal-a 14-month, $8M program involving 5 internal teams, 2 external vendors, and regulatory oversight from state insurance authorities.

**The Stakeholder Landscape:** The program had been running for 4 months when a new TPM, Amara, joined. She inherited a stakeholder map with 8 names-all executives. The program was already showing signs of stakeholder friction:

- The **Compliance team** was raising objections to technical decisions at the last minute
- The **Call Center operations team** felt blindsided by changes that affected their workflows
- One **vendor** was repeatedly asking for requirements clarification that should have been resolved weeks earlier
- The **VP of Sales** was promising features to customers that weren't in the program scope

**Amara's Stakeholder Mapping Intervention:**

**Step 1 - Cast a wider net.** Amara spent her first week identifying every stakeholder she could find. She ended up with 37 names across 8 categories:

| Category | Count | Previously Included? |
|----------|-------|---------------------|
| Executive leadership | 4 | Yes |
| Engineering leads | 5 | Partially |
| Product management | 3 | Partially |
| Operations (call center, enrollment) | 6 | No |
| Compliance & legal | 4 | Partially |
| External vendors | 4 | Partially |
| Marketing & sales | 3 | No |
| Member experience / UX | 3 | No |

**Step 2 - Map the hidden power dynamics.** Amara used the Power-Interest Grid and discovered:

- **The Compliance Director (Maria)** was low power on the org chart but high influence in practice-any compliance finding could stop the program. She'd been categorized as "Keep Informed" but should have been "Key Player." No wonder she was raising objections at the last minute-she'd never been consulted early.

- **The Call Center Director (James)** was low power and low interest until changes affected his team. At that point, his interest spiked and his ability to raise operational concerns made him effectively high power. He'd been in the "Monitor" quadrant but was actually "Keep Satisfied."

- **The VP of Sales (Raj)** was high power and high interest-but his interest was in *his* goals (selling the new portal), not in the program's constraints. He'd been treated as a Key Player but needed a different engagement approach: more time spent on "here's what we can and can't deliver."

**Step 3 - Redesign engagement strategies.** Amara created new engagement plans for the previously overlooked stakeholders:

| Stakeholder | Old Category | New Category | New Engagement |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Compliance Director | Keep Informed | Key Player | Bi-weekly architecture review attendance, early compliance consultation on all major decisions |
| Call Center Director | Monitor | Keep Satisfied | Monthly operational impact review, advance notice of all workflow changes |
| VP of Sales | Key Player | Key Player (revised approach) | Monthly roadmap update with clear scope boundaries, change request process for new features |
| Vendor Project Manager | Keep Informed | Keep Satisfied | Weekly integration sync, shared requirements document, single point of contact |

**Step 4 - Repair relationships.** Amara scheduled individual meetings with each previously under-engaged stakeholder:

**With Maria (Compliance Director):**
> "I realize we haven't been engaging you early enough in the architecture process. Going forward, I'd like to include you in our bi-weekly design reviews. Can we start this week?"

Maria was initially skeptical-"I've raised concerns 3 times and no one acted on them." Amara acknowledged the failure and committed to a specific change: compliance would be consulted before architecture decisions, not informed after.

**With James (Call Center Director):**
> "I know the recent API change affected your team's workflows without notice. I apologize. Let me walk you through the next 3 months of planned changes and get your input on timing."

James appreciated the advance notice and identified 2 changes that would have created call center chaos if implemented simultaneously.

**With Raj (VP of Sales):**
> "I've noticed customers are being promised features that aren't in the program scope. I'd like to set up a monthly scope review so you know exactly what's in the pipeline and what isn't."

Raj initially resisted-"I need flexibility to close deals." Amara showed him data: 3 features promised to customers would take 14 months to deliver. "If you promise these, customers will be disappointed. Let me help you set expectations that we can actually meet." Raj agreed to the monthly scope review.

**The Outcomes:**

- **Compliance objections dropped from 4 per month to 0** after Maria was included in early design reviews
- **Call center incidents related to the program dropped 80%** - no more surprise workflow changes
- **Sales promises aligned with program scope** - Raj stopped over-promising once he understood the constraints
- **Vendor integration delays decreased 60%** - because requirements were clear and communication was consistent
- **The program launched in Month 15** (1 month past original) but with significantly less friction than the previous 4 months

**Lessons Learned:**

1. **The initial stakeholder map was dangerously incomplete.** Amara found 29 stakeholders that the previous TPM hadn't engaged (out of 37 total). Most program stakeholder failures stem from not knowing who your stakeholders are.

2. **Power is not always on the org chart.** Maria (Compliance Director) had no direct authority over the program, but her ability to raise compliance findings gave her effective veto power. The previous TPM's org-chart-based mapping missed this.

3. **Stakeholder interest is dynamic.** James (Call Center Director) went from low-interest to high-interest whenever his team was affected. Good stakeholder management means anticipating these shifts, not reacting to them.

4. **Repairing neglected stakeholder relationships requires acknowledgment, not just action.** Amara's first step with each stakeholder was to acknowledge the previous failure: "We should have engaged you earlier." This acknowledgment was necessary before the action plan could be trusted.

5. **Sales stakeholders need special handling.** Raj wasn't malicious-he was responding to the incentives he was given (close deals). The solution wasn't to restrict him but to give him better information. The monthly scope review gave him what he needed to make promises he could keep.

# 2. Executive Reporting

## Framework

### Why Executive Reporting Is Different from Regular Communication

Communicating with executives is fundamentally different from communicating with peers or team members. Executives have:
- **Less context** - They don't live in the program details
- **Less time** - They may have 30 seconds to read your update
- **Different priorities** - They care about outcomes, risks, and decisions, not activities and tasks
- **Different decision-making authority** - They can make decisions that change your program's direction

The best TPMs master the art of executive communication because it's the primary way they build credibility, secure support, and influence decisions at the level where program-changing decisions are made.

### Research Foundation

Executive reporting draws on the principles of high-stakes communication and message design. Research in management communication shows that executives process information through pattern recognition (they look for deviations from expected patterns) and that concise, structured communication improves decision quality. The "Pyramid Principle" (Barbara Minto, 1987), developed at McKinsey, established that business communication should lead with the conclusion, followed by supporting arguments. This structure maps directly to effective TPM executive reporting: state the status, present the evidence, ask for the decision.

### The Executive Reporting Framework (4-Step Method)

#### Step 1: Understand Your Executive's Information Needs

Not all executives want the same information. In your first interaction with a new executive stakeholder, ask:

> "What do you most want to know about this program? How do you prefer to receive updates? How often? How much detail?"

Common executive preferences:

| Type | What They Care About | Format | Frequency |
|------|---------------------|--------|-----------|
| **The delegator** | Bottom line only. "Is it on track? If not, what do you need from me?" | 3-bullet email | Weekly |
| **The detailer** | Wants to understand the details. "Show me the data." | Dashboard + 1-page brief | Weekly or bi-weekly |
| **The strategist** | Strategic implications. "How does this affect our quarter? Our year? Our competitive position?" | Narrative + options | Monthly |
| **The worrier** | Risk-focused. "What could go wrong? What are you doing about it?" | Risk register + mitigation status | Weekly |

**The key:** Adapt your reporting style to the executive, don't expect them to adapt to you.

#### Step 2: Structure Your Report for Speed

**The 3-Sentence Executive Update:**

| Sentence | What It Contains | Example |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **1. The headline** | Overall status in one sentence | "The payment integration program is on track for the June 15 launch, but we have a vendor dependency that needs attention." |
| **2. The evidence** | Key data point supporting the headline | "We've completed 75% of milestones, and the core integration is ahead of schedule. However, the encryption certification from Vendor X is running 2 weeks behind." |
| **3. The ask** | What you need (or "no ask needed") | "I need you to authorize escalation to the Vendor X account executive to prioritize our certification. A 2-sentence email from you would resolve this." |

**The rule:** If your executive update takes more than 30 seconds to read, it's too long. Assume they will read the first sentence. Optimize for that.

#### Step 3: Use the RAG Status with a Narrative

The RAG (Red/Amber/Green) status is the universal executive shorthand:

| Status | Meaning | When to Use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| 🟢 **Green** | On track. No issues requiring executive attention. | Most weeks. But even "green" needs a brief narrative. |
| 🟡 **Amber** | At risk. May need executive attention if not resolved soon. | When there's a known issue with a mitigation plan. |
| 🔴 **Red** | Off track. Executive attention needed now. | When there's a significant problem requiring a decision or resource change. |

**Important:** Never hide bad news. Executives trust TPMs who surface problems early. A "red" that you caught early and are handling is better than a "green" that turns into a crisis.

**The RAG Narrative Template:**

```
Program: [Name]
Status: 🟢 GREEN / 🟡 AMBER / 🔴 RED
Date: [Date]

PROGRESS THIS WEEK:
• [Key accomplishment]
• [Key accomplishment]
• [Key milestone achieved]

RISKS TO WATCH:
• [Risk] - Mitigation: [What's being done] - Needs decision by [Date]

DECISIONS NEEDED:
• [Decision needed] - Options: [A/B/C] - TPM recommendation: [Option] - Deadline: [Date]

METRICS SNAPSHOT:
• [Key metric]: [Current] (Target: [Target])
• [Key metric]: [Current] (Target: [Target])
```

#### Step 4: Build a Regular Cadence

| Cadence | Format | Audience | Content |
|---------|--------|----------|---------|
| **Weekly** | 3-sentence email (30-second read) | Direct executive stakeholder | Headline, evidence, ask |
| **Monthly** | 1-page brief (3-minute read) | Steering committee | RAG status, key metrics, top risks, decisions needed |
| **Quarterly** | Presentation (15-minute read) | Leadership team | Strategic review, lessons learned, updated roadmap |

The weekly email is the most important. It's your primary tool for building trust and credibility with your executive stakeholder. Be consistent. Send it every week at the same time, even when there's "nothing to report."

### Why This Approach Works

The 3-sentence structure works because:
1. **Respects time.** Executives can process the update in 30 seconds and decide whether to dig deeper.
2. **Forces clarity.** If you can't state your status in 3 sentences, you don't understand it well enough.
3. **Makes the ask explicit.** Executives don't have to guess what you need from them.
4. **Builds trust.** Consistent, honest updates (including red statuses) demonstrate competence and transparency.

### Realistic Examples

#### Example 1: The Routine Weekly Update (Green)

**Situation:** Your program is on track. No major issues. You send your weekly executive update:

**To:** VP of Engineering  
**Subject:** [Weekly Update] Customer Portal Redesign - 🟢 Green

> **Status:** The Customer Portal program is on track for the September 15 launch. All 6 workstreams are progressing within their sprint commitments.
>
> **Progress this week:** Backend API integration completed ahead of schedule (92% test pass rate). Frontend team began user testing with 10 internal users-feedback is positive. The security review for the new authentication flow passed without findings.
>
> **Needs from you:** Nothing this week. We're on track for next month's steering committee review.

**Why this works:**
- Three-second status read (🟢 Green)
- Concrete evidence (API, user testing, security review)
- Clear "no ask needed" (she doesn't have to wonder if there's a hidden request)

#### Example 2: The Amber Update with a Decision Request

**Situation:** A critical vendor dependency is slipping. You have a mitigation plan but need a decision.

**To:** VP of Engineering  
**Subject:** [Weekly Update] Payment Integration - 🟡 Amber

> **Status:** The Payment Integration program is on track for the June 15 launch, but a vendor dependency has shifted from green to amber.
>
> **Key issue:** Vendor X's encryption certification is running 2 weeks behind. We've identified two options: (A) Accept the 2-week delay-launch shifts to June 29. (B) Escalate to Vendor X's executive team to prioritize our certification-keeps June 15 launch at risk but possible.
>
> **Decision needed by Friday:** Which option? TPM recommends Option B with a fallback to Option A if we don't get confirmation from Vendor X by end of next week.

**Why this works:**
- Immediate status read (🟡 Amber)
- Specific issue named (encryption certification)
- Clear options presented (A or B)
- Decision requested with deadline (Friday)
- TPM recommendation included (shows judgment)

#### Example 3: The Red Update (Needs Immediate Attention)

**Situation:** A critical security finding threatens the launch timeline. You need the VP to make a decision today.

**To:** VP of Engineering  
**Subject:** [URGENT] Payment Integration - 🔴 Red - Launch at Risk

> **Status:** The Payment Integration program is red. A security finding in the new payment processing flow requires 3–4 weeks of remediation work, which would push our June 15 launch date.
>
> **The finding:** The vulnerability assessment identified that sensitive cardholder data could be exposed in memory during processing. The fix requires encrypting data in transit between services-a 3–4 week effort. The security team will not sign off without this fix.
>
> **Decision needed today:** We have three options. (A) Delay launch by 4 weeks to fix the issue. (B) Launch on June 15 with the vulnerability and a remediation plan-requires security exception approval from you. (C) Reduce scope by removing the card-on-file feature (the source of the vulnerability) and launch on time with basic payment processing.
>
> **Recommendation:** Option C-descoping card-on-file to Phase 2. This keeps the launch date, addresses the security concern, and defers the complex feature to a follow-up program. I've confirmed with Product that card-on-file isn't required for the initial launch.

**Why this works:**
- 🔴 Red flagged clearly in subject line
- Problem stated in first sentence
- Specific impact quantified (3–4 weeks)
- Three options with tradeoffs
- TPM recommendation included
- Relevant stakeholder (Product) already consulted

### Case Study

**Organization:** A B2B SaaS company ("GridSpace") was building a new analytics platform-a 12-month, $5M program. The program was led by a TPM, Daniel, who reported to the VP of Product.

**The Problem:** Daniel had been sending the VP of Product a detailed weekly status email: 5–7 paragraphs covering each team's progress, challenges, and plans. The VP never responded. Daniel assumed this meant everything was fine.

In Month 7, the CEO asked the VP of Product: "How's the analytics platform program going?"

The VP of Product couldn't answer. She had been skim-reading Daniel's emails (or not reading them at all) because they were too long and too detailed. She had no clear mental model of the program's health.

Daniel was frustrated: "I've been sending you detailed updates every week for 7 months."

The VP responded: "I'm sorry. I should have told you sooner-I don't have time to read long emails. I need the 30-second version."

**Daniel's Executive Reporting Overhaul:**

Daniel completely restructured his executive reporting approach:

**Step 1 - Asked what the VP actually needed.** Daniel asked:

> "What are the 3 things you most want to know every week? How much detail? What format?"

The VP's answer:
1. "Is the program on track? (Green/Yellow/Red)"
2. "What's the most important thing I need to know this week?"
3. "Is there anything you need from me?"
4. Format: 5-bullet email, 30 seconds to read

**Step 2 - Redesigned the weekly email.**

**Before (7 paragraphs, 500+ words):**

> "This week, Team A completed the data pipeline integration. However, they encountered some challenges with the schema migration that required coordination with Team B. After three alignment sessions, they resolved the issue by adopting a phased migration approach. Meanwhile, Team C began their work on the dashboard UI. The designer presented two mockups, and the team is still deciding which direction to take. The vendor for the visualization library sent a revised proposal that increases our costs by 15%. I've scheduled a call with them next week to negotiate..."

**After (5 bullets, 100 words):**

> **Program: Analytics Platform - 🟢 Green**
>
> • **Progress:** Data pipeline integration complete (on schedule). Dashboard UI design in progress (on schedule).
> • **Risks:** Visualization vendor proposing 15% cost increase-negotiating next week (may need escalation).
> • **Next week:** Dashboard prototype review. Vendor negotiation follow-up.
> • **Needs:** None this week.
> • **Highlight:** Schema migration completed without customer impact-excellent cross-team coordination.

**Step 3 - Added a monthly 1-page executive brief.** In addition to the weekly 5-bullet email, Daniel created a monthly 1-page brief with:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Program health | RAG + key metrics |
| Milestone progress | % complete, milestones hit/missed |
| Top 3 risks | Current status and mitigation |
| Decisions needed | Upcoming decisions with recommendations |
| Next 30 days | Key focus areas |

**Step 4 - Built a pre-read habit.** Before the monthly steering committee, Daniel sent the 1-page brief 48 hours in advance with a note:

> "Here's the pre-read for Thursday's steering committee. Three things to note:
> 1. We're on track.
> 2. The vendor cost increase is the main discussion item.
> 3. No launch date changes needed.
>
> The meeting will focus on decision-making, not status review."

**The Outcomes:**

- **The VP of Product started reading Daniel's updates again** - and responding to them
- **The CEO got a better answer** - "The program is green. Here's the key risk we're managing. Daniel can walk you through it in 5 minutes."
- **Steering committee meetings became more efficient** - decisions instead of status updates
- **Daniel's executive credibility increased significantly** - the VP of Product recommended him for a promotion

**Lessons Learned:**

1. **Ask what executives need-don't assume.** Daniel spent 7 months sending detailed emails that the VP never read. A 5-minute conversation would have revealed the mismatch. New TPMs should always ask: "How do you prefer to receive updates?"

2. **Brevity is a sign of competence, not laziness.** Daniel's long emails actually signaled *insecurity* (trying to prove he was on top of everything). The concise updates signaled *confidence* (he knew what mattered).

3. **Bad news should be surfaced early, not buried.** The VP's trust in Daniel increased after he started clearly flagging amber and red items. Previously, she had to read between the lines. Now she could trust the status indicator at the top.

4. **No "needs" is a positive signal.** Sending "Needs: Nothing this week" was surprisingly valuable. It told the VP: "I've got this under control. You don't need to worry about this program this week."

5. **Pre-reads transform meetings.** The 48-hour pre-read for steering committee meetings eliminated the first 20 minutes of "catching up" and allowed the meeting to focus on discussion and decisions.

# 3. Steering Committees

## Framework

### What Is a Steering Committee?

A **steering committee** is a governing body of senior stakeholders who provide oversight, make key decisions, and remove obstacles for a program. It's not a status update forum-it's a **decision-making body.**

The steering committee typically includes:
- **The program sponsor** (the senior leader who funds and champions the program)
- **Key stakeholders** (VPs or directors from affected departments)
- **The TPM** (who organizes and presents to the committee)
- **Technical leads** (as needed for specific discussions)

### Research Foundation

Effective steering committees follow the principles of high-functioning governance bodies. Research on meeting effectiveness (Rogelberg et al., 2006) shows that the most common complaint about meetings is "no clear purpose" and "too much information sharing." Steering committees fail when they become status update forums instead of decision-making bodies. The key design principle is **decision-centricity:** every agenda item should lead to a decision, an action, or an explicit deferral.

### The Steering Committee Framework (5-Step Method)

#### Step 1: Design the Committee

| Design Element | Recommendation | Why |
|---------------|---------------|-----|
| **Size** | 5–8 members | Larger groups struggle to reach decisions |
| **Frequency** | Monthly (bi-weekly during critical phases) | Monthly balances oversight with cadence |
| **Duration** | 45–60 minutes | Longer meetings lose focus |
| **Membership** | Decision-makers only | If they can't make decisions for their area, they shouldn't be on the committee |
| **Chair** | Program sponsor | Gives the meeting authority |
| **Secretary** | TPM | You organize, prepare, and follow up |

#### Step 2: Structure the Agenda

A well-structured steering committee meeting follows this pattern:

| Time | Item | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| **5 min** | Welcome and decisions log review | Confirm previous decisions, note any changes |
| **10 min** | Program health (RAG + key metrics) | Shared context-not discussion |
| **15 min** | Discussion item #1 (decision needed) | The main discussion |
| **15 min** | Discussion item #2 (if needed) | Secondary discussion |
| **5 min** | Escalations and decisions summary | Confirm decisions made, action items |

**Key rule:** If an agenda item doesn't require a decision, it shouldn't be on the steering committee agenda. Status updates go in the pre-read. Steering committees are for decisions.

#### Step 3: Prepare the Pre-Read

Send a pre-read document 48 hours before the meeting:

> **Steering Committee Pre-Read - [Date]**
>
> **Program Status:** 🟢 Green
> **Key Metrics:** [Milestone completion: 78%, Risk count: 12 (2 critical), Budget: 45% spent at 50% timeline]
>
> **Discussion Items for Decision:**
> 1. **Vendor contract extension** - Current vendor's contract expires before program completion. Options for extension.
> 2. **Scope change request** - Sales requesting priority feature addition. Tradeoff analysis.
>
> **For your awareness (no decision needed):**
> • Team restructuring in progress (Q3)
> • Upcoming milestone review in 3 weeks

#### Step 4: Facilitate the Meeting

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Chair (Sponsor)** | Opens meeting, facilitates decisions, keeps time, closes with clear decisions |
| **TPM** | Presents program health, provides context for discussion items, captures decisions |
| **Members** | Bring their perspective, make decisions for their areas |

**During discussions, the TPM's role is to:**
1. Frame the decision clearly: "We need to decide X. Here are the options. Here's my recommendation."
2. Present data, not opinions: "Option A saves 2 weeks but costs $50K. Option B is on budget but adds 3 weeks."
3. Facilitate conversation without dominating: Let committee members discuss. Step in only to clarify or redirect.
4. Capture decisions explicitly: "So we've agreed to Option A. Is that correct? I'll document that and update the plan."

#### Step 5: Follow Up Within 24 Hours

After the meeting, send:
1. **Decision log update** - What was decided, who owns the action, by when
2. **Updated program plan** (if decisions affect the plan)
3. **Next meeting date and agenda preview**

### Why This Approach Works

The decision-centric meeting structure works because:
1. **Respects senior leaders' time.** They're not sitting through a 60-minute status update.
2. **Forces preparation.** Knowing you have only 15 minutes per discussion item forces you to prepare clear options.
3. **Produces clear outcomes.** Every meeting ends with documented decisions and action items.
4. **Builds TPM credibility.** When you consistently present well-structured, decision-ready materials, stakeholders trust your judgment.

### Realistic Examples

#### Example 1: The Perfect Steering Committee

**Situation:** The monthly steering committee for a cloud migration program. The TPM, Sarah, has been running these meetings for 6 months. They've become efficient and productive.

**The flow:**

> **5 min - Welcome and previous decisions:**
> "Last meeting we decided to extend Vendor A's contract by 3 months. The contract has been signed. Next item."

> **10 min - Program health:**
> Sarah presents the dashboard. "Overall green. 4 of 6 milestones on track. One at risk-the database migration is behind by 1 week. Mitigation plan is to add an extra engineer next sprint. No decision needed today unless someone objects."

> **15 min - Discussion: New compliance requirement:**
> "A new data regulation takes effect in 4 months. Our current architecture may not be compliant. Three options..."
> The committee discusses for 12 minutes and chooses Option B (architecture adjustment, adds 2 weeks to timeline). Sarah captures the decision.

> **5 min - Summary:**
> "Decision made: Option B for the compliance requirement. Action: Engineering lead will assess the 2-week impact and confirm next week. Next meeting: [Date]. Agenda preview: Phase 2 scope review."

**Meeting ends at 35 minutes.** The committee leaves with clarity. Sarah has clear decisions to implement.

#### Example 2: The Dysfunctional Steering Committee Turnaround

**Situation:** A new TPM, James, inherits a steering committee that meets for 90 minutes and makes no decisions. The meetings are:
- 30 minutes of "what happened this month" (everyone presenting their team's status)
- 30 minutes of debate about scope (no clear options presented)
- 15 minutes of "we'll need to think about that" (decisions deferred)
- 15 minutes of "any other business" (surprise topics that should have been raised before)

**James's turnaround:**

**Step 1 - Redesign the format.** James proposes a new format to the sponsor:
> "I think we can make these meetings more productive. Instead of status updates, I'll send a pre-read 48 hours ahead. The meeting will focus on 1–2 decisions. We'll aim for 45 minutes."

**Step 2 - Restructure the agenda:**
- Send pre-read with program health and decision items
- No status updates in the meeting
- Two decision items, 15 minutes each
- 5-minute wrap-up

**Step 3 - The first meeting with the new format.** There's resistance: "But I need to hear from each team." James explains: "The pre-read covers that. The meeting is for decisions we can't make async."

The first meeting is awkward. Members are used to passive listening. But by the third meeting, the new format is working. Decisions are being made. The meeting ends at 45 minutes.

**Outcome:** The sponsor tells James: "These meetings are actually useful now. We're making decisions instead of just talking."

#### Example 3: The Steering Committee That Needs a Hard Conversation

**Situation:** The program sponsor (VP of Engineering) has been missing steering committee meetings. The meetings are losing authority because she's not there to make decisions.

**The TPM's approach:** James schedules a 1:1 with the VP:

> "I've noticed you've missed the last 2 steering committee meetings. I understand you're busy. But we made 3 decisions in those meetings, and 2 of them need your sign-off. Could we either (a) schedule the meetings at a time that works for you, or (b) designate a delegate who can make decisions in your absence?"

The VP agrees to Option A and they reschedule to a time she can consistently attend. James also establishes a clear policy: if the VP can't attend, she designates a delegate with decision authority.

### Case Study

**Organization:** A financial services company ("TrustBank") was modernizing its core banking platform-a 24-month, $12M program involving 10+ teams and 4 external vendors. The steering committee included the CTO, the Head of Retail Banking, the Head of Risk, the CFO, and the program sponsor (the Chief Digital Officer).

**The Problem:** The steering committee had been meeting for 9 months and was widely considered ineffective. Key issues:

1. **Meetings ran 2+ hours** with no clear outcomes
2. **The CTO dominated every discussion** - other members stopped contributing
3. **Decisions were rarely made** - most items were "taken offline" and never resolved
4. **The pre-read (when it existed) was 30+ slides** - no one read it
5. **The TPM (before the current one) had been using the meeting as a status dump** - not a decision forum

**The New TPM's Intervention:** A TPM, Anika, took over the program at Month 10. Her first observation after attending one steering committee: "This meeting is a waste of everyone's time. I need to fix this."

**Step 1 - Interview all committee members (1 week).** Anika met individually with each member:

| Member | What They Said | What It Revealed |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| **CTO** | "I don't need to hear status. I need to make technical decisions." | Current meeting was frustrating him too |
| **Head of Retail** | "I never know what's being decided until after the meeting." | Lack of communication outside the meeting |
| **Head of Risk** | "I have concerns about the vendor selection but no one asks me." | Her voice wasn't being heard in the CTO-dominated sessions |
| **CFO** | "I don't need to be at these meetings. Just tell me when the budget changes." | Wrong person at the table |
| **Sponsor (CDO)** | "I know these meetings aren't working, but I don't know what to do about it." | Ready for change |

**Step 2 - Redesign the committee (2 weeks).** Based on her interviews, Anika made three changes:

**Change 1: Reshape membership.**
- Remove: CFO (doesn't need to be there-budget decisions go through a separate process)
- Add: Head of Engineering Operations (needed for resource decisions)
- Keep: CTO, Head of Retail, Head of Risk, CDO (sponsor)

**Change 2: Restructure the format.**

| Before (2-hour status dump) | After (45-minute decision meeting) |
|-----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| 20-slide status presentation | 3-page pre-read sent 48 hours ahead |
| Open discussion, no time limits | 2 decision items, 15 minutes each |
| Decisions "taken offline" | Decisions made, documented, communicated |
| TPM presents everything | TPM facilitates, members discuss |

**Change 3: Establish ground rules.**

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| Pre-read sent 48 hours ahead, must be read before the meeting | No status in the meeting |
| Each discussion item has a clear decision to make | No open-ended conversations |
| Every discussion includes: options, recommendation, tradeoffs | Structured decision-making |
| If a decision can't be made in 15 minutes, it's deferred with a clear owner and deadline | Prevents endless debate |
| Decisions are documented and communicated within 24 hours | Creates accountability |

**Step 3 - The first redesigned meeting (Month 11).**

Anika sent the pre-read 48 hours ahead:

> **Steering Committee - Month 11**
>
> **Status:** 🟡 Amber (vendor integration delay)
>
> **Decision 1: Vendor B contract extension (15 min)**
> Current vendor's contract expires before program completion. Options:
> - A: Extend current contract - $45K, no transition risk
> - B: Switch to new vendor - $30K, but 6-week transition delay
> - TPM recommends: Option A (cost is worth avoiding the delay)
>
> **Decision 2: Phase 2 scope freeze (15 min)**
> Proposal to freeze Phase 2 scope to prevent further scope creep. Options:
> - A: Freeze now - prevents changes, may miss late-breaking requirements
> - B: Freeze at end of next month - allows critical additions, risk of more creep
> - TPM recommends: Option A with exception process for truly critical additions

The meeting lasted 42 minutes. Both decisions were made (Option A for both). The CTO participated but didn't dominate. The Head of Risk shared her perspective on the vendor decision and was heard.

**The Outcomes (Months 11–24):**

- **Steering committee meetings averaged 44 minutes** (down from 120+)
- **Decision completion rate increased from 30% to 90%** (decisions made vs. deferred)
- **The CTO's dominance decreased** - the structured format gave everyone space to contribute
- **The Head of Risk became an active contributor** - her early concerns about the vendor were addressed before they became problems
- **The program launched in Month 23** (1 month before the 24-month deadline)
- **The redesign was documented as a best practice** for the organization

**Lessons Learned:**

1. **The pre-read is the most important document.** If committee members read the pre-read, the meeting can focus on decisions. If they don't, you waste the first 20 minutes catching everyone up. Anika made pre-read quality her top priority.

2. **Not everyone belongs at every meeting.** The CFO didn't need to attend monthly-quarterly budget reviews were sufficient. Removing her made the meeting smaller and faster. She appreciated the time savings.

3. **The TPM's role is facilitate, not dominate.** Anika's previous steering committee had the TPM presenting for 40 minutes. Anika's redesigned meeting had the TPM speaking for 10 minutes and facilitating for 35. The committee members did the talking.

4. **Every decision needs options, not just a request.** Presenting "We need to decide whether to extend Vendor B's contract" invites open-ended debate. Presenting "Option A costs $45K, Option B costs $30K with a 6-week delay. TPM recommends Option A" structures the decision and accelerates the conversation.

5. **Follow-up within 24 hours is non-negotiable.** Anika's decision log email, sent within 4 hours of every meeting, created accountability and closed the loop. Committee members knew their decisions would be documented and acted on.

# 4. Conflict Resolution

## Framework

### Why Conflict Resolution Is a Core TPM Competency

TPMs operate at the intersection of multiple teams, departments, and stakeholders. Naturally, these groups have different priorities, perspectives, and pressures. Conflict is not a sign that something is wrong-it's a sign that people care about their work. The question is how you handle it.

A TPM who can resolve conflict effectively:
- Prevents small disagreements from becoming program-blocking disputes
- Maintains relationships that survive difficult decisions
- Creates an environment where people feel heard, even when they don't get what they want
- Saves weeks of program time that would otherwise be lost to unresolved tension

### Research Foundation

Conflict resolution draws on the "Interest-Based Relational Approach" (Fisher & Ury, 1981) from the Harvard Negotiation Project. This approach distinguishes between **positions** (what people say they want) and **interests** (why they want it). Effective conflict resolution focuses on interests, not positions. Additional research on workplace conflict (CPP Global Human Capital Report, 2008) found that U.S. employees spend 2.8 hours per week dealing with conflict-and that trained conflict resolution skills significantly reduce this time while improving outcomes.

### The Conflict Resolution Framework (5-Step Method)

#### Step 1: Diagnose the Conflict Type

Not all conflicts are the same. Different types need different approaches:

| Type | What It Looks Like | Example | Approach |
|------|-------------------|---------|----------|
| **Substantive** | Disagreement about facts, plans, or decisions | Two teams disagree on which vendor to use | Data, analysis, structured decision-making |
| **Procedural** | Disagreement about how decisions are made | "You should have consulted us before making that decision" | Process clarification, decision rights |
| **Relational** | Personal friction, trust issues, communication breakdowns | Two team leads who don't trust each other | Relationship rebuilding, facilitated conversation |
| **Resource** | Competing demands for limited resources | Two teams need the same engineer for different projects | Tradeoff analysis, prioritization |

**Start by identifying what type of conflict you're dealing with.** Applying the wrong approach (e.g., trying to solve a relational conflict with data) will make things worse.

#### Step 2: Separate Positions from Interests

This is the single most important skill in conflict resolution.

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Position** | What someone says they want | "We need the senior engineer assigned to our team for the next 3 months" |
| **Interest** | Why they want it | "We have a critical deadline and we're worried about missing it" |

**To surface interests, ask:**
- "Help me understand why this matters to you."
- "What would having this allow you to do?"
- "What are you worried would happen if you didn't get this?"

**Example:**

> **Two team leads are in conflict over a shared resource:**
>
> *Team Lead A (position):* "We need Sarah for the next 3 sprints."
> *Team Lead B (position):* "No, we need Sarah for the next 3 sprints."
>
> *You (surfacing interests):* "Let me understand what's driving this. Aisha, what's the concern if Sarah isn't on your team?"
> *Aisha:* "We have a regulatory deadline. If we miss it, we face penalties."
> *Brett:* "We have a customer launch. If we delay, the client is threatening to cancel."
>
> **The real conflict:** Both teams have hard deadlines. The "position" conflict (who gets Sarah) is about a deeper "interest" conflict (both have critical deadlines they can't miss). The solution might not be about Sarah at all-it could be about finding alternative resources, adjusting deadlines, or splitting Sarah's time.

#### Step 3: Create a Safe Environment for Resolution

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Meet separately with each party first | Hold the resolution conversation in a group setting initially |
| Acknowledge each perspective as valid | Dismiss concerns or take sides |
| Focus on the problem, not the person | Blame or characterize ("You always...") |
| Use "I" statements and neutral language | Use accusatory language |
| Agree on a shared goal upfront | Let the conversation drift without purpose |

**Opening script for a conflict resolution conversation:**

> "Thank you both for making time. I know this issue has been frustrating. My goal in this conversation is to find a solution that works for both teams and for the program. I'm not here to take sides. Let's start by each of you sharing your perspective-what's most important to you in this situation?"

#### Step 4: Generate Options Together

After surfacing interests, work with both parties to generate options:

| Approach | How It Works | Best For |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Expand the pie** | Find more resources, time, or options | Resource conflicts |
| **Trade across priorities** | Each party gives on what matters less to them, gains on what matters more | Priority conflicts |
| **Structure a compromise** | Each party gives up something | When no creative solution exists |
| **Find a third path** | Neither party's original solution-a new option neither had considered | Stalemates |

**Brainstorming with conflicting parties:**

> "Based on what I'm hearing, the core challenge is that both teams have critical deadlines in the same timeframe. Let's think creatively. What if we could bring in a contractor for one team? What if we could shift one deadline? What if we split Sarah's time 60/40 for 2 weeks and then full-time to the other team?"

#### Step 5: Agree on a Solution and Document It

Once a solution is reached:
1. **Confirm agreement explicitly:** "So we've agreed that Sarah will spend 60% of her time with Team A for the next 2 weeks, then full-time with Team B. Is that correct?"
2. **Document:** Write down the decision, who agreed, and any commitments.
3. **Follow up:** Check in after 1 week to ensure the solution is working.

### Why This Approach Works

The Interest-Based approach works because:
1. **Positions are rigid; interests are flexible.** A person who won't budge from "we need Sarah for 3 months" may be open to "we need someone with Sarah's skills for this specific task for 2 weeks."
2. **Understanding interests reveals hidden solutions.** Once you know the *real* concern (regulatory deadline, customer threat), you can address it directly rather than fighting about the stated position.
3. **Joint problem-solving preserves relationships.** When you resolve conflicts collaboratively, both parties feel heard and respected. The relationship survives the disagreement.

### Realistic Examples

#### Example 1: Substantive Conflict - Two Teams Disagree on Technology Choice

**Situation:** The Frontend team wants to use a new React framework. The Backend team wants to stick with the existing framework to avoid additional integration complexity. The teams are stuck, and the deadline is approaching.

**Step 1 - Diagnose:**
- Type: Substantive (disagreement about technical approach)
- Each team has valid concerns

**Step 2 - Surface interests:**
> **To Frontend lead:** "Help me understand why the new framework matters to your team."
> "It would reduce our development time by 40% and make the UI significantly better."
>
> **To Backend lead:** "What's your concern with the new framework?"
> "We'd have to rewrite our integration layer. That's 6 weeks of work we don't have."

**The real interests:**
- Frontend: Faster development, better product
- Backend: Avoiding unplanned work, minimizing risk

**Step 3 - Generate options:**
- Option A: New framework. Frontend wins, but Backend takes 6-week hit.
- Option B: Current framework. Backend wins, Frontend slower.
- Option C: New framework with Backend integration delayed (Frontend works with mock data, Backend integrates later).
- Option D: Current framework with dedicated Frontend time to optimize (trade-off: slower Frontend, no Backend impact).

**Step 4 - Agree on solution:**
The teams choose Option C: New framework, Backend integration deferred by 1 sprint, Frontend builds with mock data in the meantime. The compromise saves 4 weeks for Backend while giving Frontend the framework they want.

#### Example 2: Procedural Conflict - "You Should Have Consulted Us"

**Situation:** The Platform team made a technical decision that affects the Data team. The Data team found out through a Slack message and is upset: "You made a decision that affects our systems without consulting us!"

**The TPM's approach:**

**Step 1 - Acknowledge the concern:**
> "I understand why you're frustrated. The decision affects your team's systems, and you weren't included in the conversation. That shouldn't have happened."

**Step 2 - Understand the procedural gap:**
> "Let's figure out how to fix this going forward. What decisions should the Platform team consult the Data team on? And what's the best way for that to happen?"

**Step 3 - Establish a decision rights agreement:**
The two teams agree:
- Platform team can make minor technical decisions independently (no Data impact)
- Platform team must consult Data team on decisions affecting data storage, processing, or APIs
- Consultation happens through a shared Slack channel with a 48-hour response window

The agreement is documented. The same conflict doesn't recur.

#### Example 3: Relational Conflict - Two Team Leads Who Don't Trust Each Other

**Situation:** Two engineering leads, Maria (Frontend) and David (Backend), have a history of conflict. Maria accuses David of changing APIs without notice. David accuses Maria of not reading his documentation. The tension is affecting team morale and delivery.

**Step 1 - Meet individually with each:**
You meet with Maria and David separately to understand their perspectives without the other person present.

- **Maria's view:** "David makes changes and doesn't tell anyone. I find out when my tests break."
- **David's view:** "I write detailed documentation. Maria doesn't read it and then blames me."

**Step 2 - Identify the systemic issue:**
The individual meetings reveal that the problem isn't bad intent-it's a process gap. David documents changes in a place Maria doesn't check. Maria expects changes to be announced in Slack, which David doesn't use for technical communication.

**Step 3 - Facilitate a joint problem-solving conversation:**
You bring them together with a shared goal: "Let's find a way that API changes get communicated reliably, without either of you having to change your working style."

They agree on:
- David posts API change notices in a dedicated Slack channel (#api-changes)
- Maria (or her team member) monitors that channel
- For breaking changes, David tags Maria directly

**Step 4 - Follow up:**
After 2 weeks, you check in with both. The system is working. More importantly, the act of solving a problem together has improved their relationship. They start communicating directly rather than through you.

### Case Study

**Organization:** A media streaming company ("StreamFlix") was building a new recommendation engine-a 6-month program involving the Data Science team, the Backend Engineering team, and the Product team.

**The Conflict:** Two months into the program, a significant conflict erupted between the Data Science team and the Backend Engineering team.

**The positions:**
- **Data Science (Dr. Singh, lead):** "We need direct write access to the production user database to train our recommendation model on real-time data."
- **Backend Engineering (Tom, lead):** "Absolutely not. Direct write access to production from an external process is a security and stability risk. We've said no three times."

**The situation escalated:**
- Dr. Singh went to the VP of Engineering and argued that Backend Engineering was blocking the program
- Tom went to the same VP and argued that Data Science was being reckless
- The VP told them to "work it out" - but they couldn't
- Work on the recommendation engine stopped for 2 weeks
- The product launch date was at risk

**The TPM's Intervention:**

The TPM, Amara, stepped in when the conflict had been deadlocked for 2 weeks.

**Step 1 - Separate meetings to understand interests.**

**With Dr. Singh (Data Science):**
> "Help me understand why you need production data access."
>
> *Interest surfaced:* "Our model needs to learn from real user behavior patterns. Sample data from last month doesn't capture the new user cohorts we just onboarded. Without real-time production data, the model's accuracy drops by 30%."

**With Tom (Backend Engineering):**
> "What specific concern do you have about production access?"
>
> *Interest surfaced:* "A bug in the model's training pipeline could corrupt production data. We'd have a site-wide outage. I've seen this happen at my previous company. It took 3 days to recover."

**Step 2 - Reframe the problem.**
Amara identified that the real conflict wasn't about technical access-it was about **risk tolerance.** Dr. Singh wanted model accuracy (which required real-time data). Tom wanted stability (which required isolation). Both were legitimate interests.

**Step 3 - Bring them together with a structured conversation.**

Amara scheduled a 60-minute meeting with both leads. She started:

> "I want to acknowledge both perspectives. Dr. Singh, the model's accuracy depends on real-time data-that's a real program risk. Tom, a production outage is a real platform risk. Neither of you is wrong. Let's find a solution that addresses both concerns."

**Step 4 - Generate options together.**

| Option | Pros | Cons | Acceptability |
|--------|------|------|---------------|
| A. Direct production access (Dr. Singh's position) | Best model accuracy | High outage risk | Unacceptable to Tom |
| B. No production access (Tom's position) | Safe | Model accuracy suffers 30% | Unacceptable to Dr. Singh |
| C. Read-only replica with near real-time sync | Low risk, fairly current data | Data may be 5-10 minutes delayed | Worth exploring |
| D. Sandboxed write environment (writes to a separate table, replicated to production after validation) | No outage risk, real-time training data | Engineering effort to build sandbox (2 weeks) | Best compromise |

**Step 5 - Choose and commit.**

The teams chose Option D: a sandboxed write environment. Data Science could write to a separate table that was merged into production after automated validation checks. Backend Engineering would build the validation pipeline (2 weeks of work).

Tom committed to building the validation pipeline. Dr. Singh committed to accepting the 2-week delay and testing the sandbox environment.

**The Outcomes:**

- **The sandbox was built in 2 weeks** and worked as designed
- **Model accuracy reached 94%** (compared to 96% with direct access-3% sacrifice for 100% risk elimination)
- **The program launched on schedule** (the 2 weeks were absorbed by adjusting other priorities)
- **Tom and Dr. Singh developed mutual respect** - Tom later said: "Dr. Singh's team found 3 edge cases in our validation pipeline we hadn't considered." Dr. Singh said: "I understand why Tom was worried. The sandbox was the right call."
- **The sandbox approach** was adopted for future Data Science projects

**What Would Have Happened Without Intervention:**

The conflict would have escalated to the VP of Engineering again. The VP would have needed to make a decision (likely siding with Backend Engineering on safety grounds), damaging the Data Science team's engagement. Model quality would have suffered, and the recommendation engine might have launched late or with subpar performance.

**Lessons Learned:**

1. **Conflict isn't personal-it's about unmet interests.** Dr. Singh and Tom weren't being difficult. They were advocating for legitimate concerns: accuracy and stability. The TPM's job was to surface those underlying interests and find a solution that addressed both.

2. **The best solutions often come from neither party's original position.** Options C and D (the creative solutions) weren't proposed by either side. They emerged from the structured conversation that the TPM facilitated.

3. **Escalation to a higher authority should be a last resort, not a first response.** Dr. Singh and Tom had both gone to the VP, who sent them back without resolution. This wasted 2 weeks. Amara's facilitated conversation resolved the issue in 60 minutes.

4. **Acknowledging both perspectives builds trust in the facilitator.** Amara's opening statement ("Neither of you is wrong") was deliberate. It established that she wasn't taking sides, which made both parties willing to engage in collaborative problem-solving.

5. **Document the agreement.** Amara wrote a one-page summary of the decision, including the validation pipeline timeline and the acceptance criteria. Both leads signed off. This prevented any "I thought we agreed to X" conversations later.

# 5. Negotiation

## Framework

### Why Negotiation Is a Daily TPM Practice

Every day as a TPM, you negotiate:
- **Scope:** "Can you add this feature and still make the deadline?"
- **Resources:** "Can your team spare an engineer for 2 weeks?"
- **Priorities:** "This initiative is more important than yours this quarter."
- **Timelines:** "Can we move the milestone by 2 weeks?"
- **Trade-offs:** "If I give you X, can you give me Y?"

These aren't formal negotiations with contracts and mediators. They're everyday conversations where you and another party have different needs and need to find a mutually acceptable path forward.

### Research Foundation

Negotiation theory is grounded in the "Harvard Negotiation Model" (Fisher, Ury & Patton, 1981) which established the principles of principled negotiation: separate people from problems, focus on interests not positions, generate options for mutual gain, and insist on objective criteria. The BATNA concept (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) is particularly relevant for TPMs-understanding your alternatives gives you leverage and clarity. Additional research on daily workplace negotiation (Kolb & Williams, 2000) shows that effective negotiators negotiate collaboratively rather than competitively, building relationships that pay dividends across multiple interactions.

### The Negotiation Framework (5-Step Method)

#### Step 1: Know What You Want-and What Your BATNA Is

Before any negotiation, clarify:

| Question | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **What is my ideal outcome?** | "The Platform team contributes 1 engineer for 4 weeks" |
| **What is my acceptable range?** | "1 engineer for 2 weeks minimum" |
| **What is my walk-away?** | "Platform team provides 2 weeks of consulting support" |
| **What is my BATNA?** (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) | "If they can't help, I'll hire a contractor. It costs $15K but takes 3 weeks" |

**Knowing your BATNA is your strongest source of leverage.** If you know your alternative, you know when to walk away-and walking away is sometimes the best negotiation move.

#### Step 2: Understand Their Position and Interests

Before the conversation, ask yourself:
- **What do they want?** (Their stated position)
- **What do they need?** (Their underlying interests)
- **What pressures are they under?** (Their constraints)
- **What is their BATNA?** (What happens if they don't get what they want from you)

**Example - Negotiating with a team lead for additional engineer resources:**

| Their stated position | "We can't spare anyone. We're already understaffed." |
|----------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| **Their interests** | Meet their own deadlines, don't burn out their team, maintain morale |
| **Their pressures** | VP asking for faster delivery on their project |
| **Their BATNA** | Keep their engineer, the program slips (not their problem) |

#### Step 3: Create Value Before Claiming It

The best negotiations aren't zero-sum. Look for ways to expand the pie before dividing it.

| Strategy | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Find complementary resources** | "I can't give you a senior engineer, but I can give you a junior engineer and a senior's part-time support" |
| **Trade across time** | "I can help you this sprint if you help me next sprint" |
| **Trade across priorities** | "Your team needs testing support. Our QA team has capacity next week." |
| **Identify unused resources** | "Does anyone on your team have bandwidth between projects? Even 2 days would help." |

#### Step 4: Structure the Exchange

Use a simple framework:

> "If you [give me X], I can [give you Y], and here's how that benefits you."

**Example:**
> "If your team can contribute one engineer for 2 weeks, I can re-prioritize our shared backlog to include the feature your stakeholders have been requesting. And your engineer will learn our system architecture, which will make future integrations faster."

**This works because:**
1. It's explicit about the exchange
2. It shows how they benefit
3. It's specific about duration and deliverables

#### Step 5: Confirm and Document

After reaching agreement:
- **Confirm explicitly:** "So we've agreed that your team will contribute 1 engineer for 2 weeks, starting Monday, and I'll prioritize the reporting feature for your stakeholders. Is that correct?"
- **Document in writing:** A short email summarizing the agreement
- **Follow up:** Check in after the agreed period to ensure both sides fulfilled their commitments

### Why This Approach Works

Principled negotiation works because:
1. **Separating interests from positions creates options.** When you know what someone *really* needs, you can often find a solution that neither party initially considered.
2. **Knowing your BATNA prevents bad deals.** If you know you can hire a contractor for $15K, you won't accept a deal that costs more than that.
3. **Creating mutual value builds relationships.** Negotiators who focus on "expanding the pie" rather than "splitting the pie" build trust and goodwill that pays off in future interactions.

### Realistic Examples

#### Example 1: Negotiating for More Engineering Resources

**Situation:** Your program needs a senior engineer from the Platform team for 4 weeks. The Platform lead says they can't spare anyone.

**Your preparation:**

| Question | Your Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| Ideal outcome | 1 senior engineer for 4 weeks |
| Acceptable range | 1 mid-level engineer for 3 weeks |
| Walk-away | Platform team provides 20 hours of consultation spread over 4 weeks |
| BATNA | Hire a contractor ($12K, 3-week ramp-up time) |

**The conversation:**

> **You:** "I know your team is stretched. Here's the situation: our program needs a senior engineer to complete the authentication integration. Without it, the program slips by 6 weeks."
>
> **Platform lead:** "I genuinely don't have anyone. We're already understaffed."
>
> **You:** "I understand. What if we did something different-what if you contributed a mid-level engineer for 3 weeks, and I provided a senior engineer from my program to help with your team's current project for the following 2 weeks?"
>
> **Platform lead:** "Hmm. That could work. Let me check with my team."
>
> **Outcome:** The Platform lead agrees to contribute a mid-level engineer for 3 weeks. In exchange, you commit a senior engineer to help the Platform team with their authentication project for 2 weeks after your program's integration is done. Both teams get what they need.

#### Example 2: Negotiating a Timeline Extension

**Situation:** Your team needs an additional 2 weeks to complete testing. The Product lead is resistant because they've already communicated the launch date to customers.

**The conversation:**

> **You:** "I need to request a 2-week extension on the launch. Here's why: we've identified 5 critical edge cases in the payment flow that we need to test. Launching without testing them would put customer transactions at risk."
>
> **Product lead:** "I can't move the date. We've already told 3 enterprise customers we'll launch on the 15th."
>
> **You:** "I understand. Let me run a few options by you:
>
> Option A: We launch on the 15th with the untested payment flow and a customer-facing warning that the feature is in 'beta.' Higher risk, but keeps the date.
>
> Option B: We launch on the 15th but disable the payment flow, launch it 2 weeks later. Customers get the rest of the product on time.
>
> Option C: We delay the full launch by 2 weeks. You communicate the delay to customers with a specific rationale: 'We're adding extra security testing to ensure your transactions are safe.'"
>
> **Product lead:** "I can't accept Option A-too much risk. Option B means the core feature is missing at launch. Option C is actually the cleanest message to customers."
>
> **Outcome:** Option C is chosen. The Product lead communicates the 2-week delay to customers framed as "extra security testing." Customers respond positively. The extra 2 weeks allow the team to complete all testing with zero critical issues at launch.

#### Example 3: Negotiating Competing Priorities

**Situation:** Two programs need the same QA team. Your program needs 3 weeks of QA starting next week. The other program has the same request. The QA lead tells you: "I only have capacity for one program at a time."

**Your approach:**

> **You:** "I understand you can only support one program at a time. Can I suggest a split: could you allocate 2 weeks to my program, then 2 weeks to the other program? My program's testing is front-loaded; after 2 weeks, I can release you. The other program's testing needs to happen later anyway because their development finishes behind mine."
>
> **QA lead:** "That actually works. Let me confirm with the other program that they're okay with starting 2 weeks later."
>
> **Outcome:** The split arrangement is agreed. Your program gets 2 weeks of QA starting next week. The other program gets 2 weeks starting 2 weeks later. Both programs' needs are met.

### Case Study

**Organization:** A SaaS company ("DocuTrack") was building a new document management platform. The program involved 4 engineering teams and had a 12-month timeline. At Month 8, a significant negotiation challenge emerged.

**The Situation:** The program was on track, but the Security team had recently instituted a new policy: all new code must undergo a security review before deployment. The Security team had only 3 reviewers for the entire engineering organization (40+ engineers across 8 programs).

For your program, this meant: each feature would need 1–2 weeks of security review before it could be deployed. Since your program had planned to release features incrementally, this would add 4–6 weeks to your timeline.

**The Stakeholders:**
| Party | Position | Interest |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **You (TPM)** | Need security reviews completed within 1 week per feature | Program must launch on time |
| **Security Lead** | "We can't expedite your reviews-we have 8 programs ahead of you" | Ensuring security quality, not burning out their 3 reviewers |
| **Your Program Sponsor** | "Figure out a way to make this work" | Launch on time |

**Your Negotiation Preparation:**

| Question | Your Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| Ideal outcome | Security reviews completed within 3 business days per feature |
| Acceptable range | Reviews within 5 business days |
| Walk-away | Accept 4–6 week delay and escalate to VP of Engineering |
| BATNA | Escalate to VP of Engineering to prioritize your program over others |

**Understanding the Security team's interests:**
- They're overwhelmed (3 reviewers for 8 programs)
- They're worried about quality (if they rush, they might miss something)
- They're measured on "findings per review" – faster reviews mean fewer findings, which looks bad

**The negotiation conversation:**

> **You:** "I understand the security review policy. I'm not asking you to skip reviews-I'm asking if we can find a way to complete them faster."
>
> **Security Lead:** "We're already stretched thin. Everyone wants their reviews expedited. I can't prioritize you over everyone else."
>
> **You:** "I get that. What if we approached this differently? Instead of requesting priority treatment, I'd like to propose a partnership.
>
> Here's the idea: I'll have my engineering team prepare the security review packages in advance-architecture diagrams, data flow documentation, threat models-before the code is ready for review. This way, when we submit for review, your team has everything they need and can complete the review in 2–3 days instead of 1–2 weeks.
>
> In exchange, I need: a commitment that reviews for properly prepared packages will be completed within 3 business days. And I need your team to provide a template for what a 'properly prepared' package looks like."
>
> **Security Lead:** "That actually changes things. Most teams submit incomplete packages and we spend days chasing documentation. If your teams come prepared, that saves us significant time. I can commit to 3 business days for well-prepared packages.
>
> The template exists. I'll share it with your team today. If your team follows it, we'll prioritize your reviews."
>
> **You:** "Thank you. One more request: can I send one of my engineers to shadow a security review? That way we can learn to prepare better packages and reduce the back-and-forth."
>
> **Security Lead:** "That's fine. Let me know who."
>
> **Outcome:**
> - The engineering team adopted the security review template
> - One engineer shadowed a security review to understand the process
> - Security reviews for well-prepared packages averaged 2.5 business days (vs. 7–14 days previously)
> - The program launched on schedule
> - The Security team adopted the "prepared package" approach for all programs, reducing their overall review backlog

**What Would Have Happened Without Negotiation:**

Your program would have escalated to the VP of Engineering, who might have forced the Security team to prioritize your program. This would have damaged the relationship between the program and Security, and the Security team would have been resentful. Future reviews might have been technically "on time" but performed grudgingly and with less thoroughness.

**Lessons Learned:**

1. **Create value before claiming it.** The TPM didn't just ask for faster reviews. She created a solution that helped both parties: her team would prepare better packages (saving the Security team time) in exchange for faster reviews.

2. **Understand the other party's constraints.** The Security team wasn't being difficult. They were overwhelmed and worried about quality. The TPM's solution addressed both concerns: better packages meant less chasing for documentation, and the shadow engineer ensured quality wasn't sacrificed.

3. **Your BATNA gives you confidence.** Knowing she could escalate to the VP of Engineering gave the TPM confidence. But she didn't use it. A good negotiation is one where the BATNA is strong enough that you can walk away, but the agreement is good enough that you don't have to.

4. **Small concessions build goodwill.** The TPM's offer to send an engineer to shadow a review was a small concession that signaled: "I'm not just asking for favors. I want to understand your work and improve our partnership." This small gesture had outsized impact on the relationship.

5. **Document the agreement.** The TPM sent a follow-up email confirming the agreement: "1. Security team provides package template. 2. Program team prepares complete packages. 3. Security team commits to 3 business day turnaround for complete packages." Both sides had a clear reference.

# Conclusion: Bringing It All Together

These five stakeholder management and communication frameworks work together in your daily practice:

| Framework | You Use It... | To Achieve... |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Stakeholder Mapping** | At program start and throughout | Understanding who matters and how to engage them |
| **Executive Reporting** | Weekly and monthly | Building credibility and securing support |
| **Steering Committees** | Monthly | Making decisions with governance |
| **Conflict Resolution** | When disagreements arise | Maintaining relationships while solving problems |
| **Negotiation** | In every cross-team interaction | Getting what you need while giving value |

**How they flow in practice:**

1. **Stakeholder mapping** tells you who to engage and how
2. **Executive reporting** keeps your key stakeholders informed and aligned
3. **Steering committees** formalize governance for major decisions
4. **Conflict resolution** handles the disagreements that inevitably arise between stakeholders
5. **Negotiation** helps you navigate the daily trade-offs required to keep the program moving

### A Beginner's Learning Path

**Month 1:** Start with **Stakeholder Mapping.** Map your current program's stakeholders. Identify at least one stakeholder you've been under-engaging and adjust your engagement.

**Month 2:** Implement **Executive Reporting.** Design your weekly update format. Ask your executive stakeholder what they need. Send consistent, concise updates.

**Month 3:** Practice **Negotiation** in daily interactions. Before your next cross-team conversation, identify your BATNA and their interests. Try to create value before dividing it.

**Month 4:** Learn **Conflict Resolution.** The next time a conflict arises between teams, use the Interest-Based approach. Surface interests before positions. Let the conflict resolution be the framework you practice this month.

**Month 5:** Master **Steering Committees.** If you run a steering committee, redesign the agenda. If you don't, observe one and identify what could be improved.

### The Most Important Thing

Stakeholder management is not about manipulation or politics. It's about **building genuine relationships** based on understanding, respect, and mutual benefit. The frameworks in this guide are tools to help you do that systematically-but they work best when applied with authentic intent.

The best TPMs are trusted by their stakeholders because they:
- Tell the truth, especially when it's hard
- Listen more than they speak
- Deliver on their commitments
- Make their stakeholders successful
- Navigate conflict with empathy and skill

These are not techniques. They're habits built through practice.
