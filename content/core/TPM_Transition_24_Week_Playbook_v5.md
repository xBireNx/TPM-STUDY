## 2. Phase 1 - TPM Foundation (Months 1–3)

### ⚡ Two Tracks. Run Both in Parallel.

The default TPM transition roadmap looks like this:

```
Learn → Apply → Get TPM Job
```

That produces candidates who say: *"I've been studying RAID logs."*

This roadmap adds a second track running **from this Monday**:

```
Learn → Start Running TPM Processes at Relevance Lab
      → Collect Real Evidence
      → Apply
```

That produces practitioners who say: *"I maintain a live RAID log for a 4-team program at Relevance Lab. Here's a blocker I cleared with it last sprint."*

Interviewers cannot dispute lived evidence. A certificate is a credential. Evidence is proof.

### Internal TPM Conversion Track - Start This Monday

You don't need a new job to start doing TPM work. You need to apply TPM processes to work you're **already doing** at Relevance Lab - Sally, CDL, or any active sprint. No one's permission required. You're a Senior Engineer. Just start.

#### The Three Actions - This Week

**Action 1 - Create a RAID Log for Sally (30 minutes)**
Open Section 18.2. Fill it with Sally's current state: every dependency, every blocker, every assumption being made. Maintain it going forward. Bring it up in your next standup as "I've been maintaining a dependency and risk tracker - here's the current state." Most engineers don't do this. You now do.

**Action 2 - Send One Executive Update This Week (20 minutes)**
Use Section 18.6. Write a one-pager for Sally or CDL. Send it to your manager or team lead. You don't need to wait to be asked - send it and say: *"I've been working on structured program communication - here's a status update for Sally."* This single email creates written evidence that someone received and acknowledged your TPM work.

**Action 3 - Run a Dependency Tracker for Your Current Sprint (20 minutes)**
Open Section 18.3. Fill it in for any cross-team dependencies in your current sprint - Frontend/Backend, Infra, QA, whatever exists right now. Share it in the next standup as a living doc. This takes 20 minutes to create and is indefinitely maintainable.

#### Evidence Collection - Do This Weekly

Start a **Wins Journal** - a Google Doc or Notion page, private, dated entries:

```
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Program: Sally / CDL / Other
Action: [What you did - RAID log updated, status report sent, blocker escalated]
Outcome: [What changed - dependency unblocked, decision made, delay prevented]
```

This journal directly feeds: Section 19 case study data, resume bullets, STAR stories, and your Month 12 performance review. Start it today, not after you get the job.

#### What You Can Say in Interviews by Month 3

> *"For the past 3 months, I've been applying TPM processes to my current programs at Relevance Lab - maintaining a RAID log, sending weekly RAG status reports to stakeholders, and tracking cross-team dependencies. I didn't wait for the title to start behaving like a TPM."*

That single statement separates you from 90% of SWE-to-TPM candidates who are still in learning mode.

### Month 1 - Build the Foundation

#### Week 1–2: Understand the TPM Role

**What a TPM actually does (internalize this):**
- Owns the *delivery* of a technical program, not the code
- Coordinates across engineering, product, design, QA, ops
- Manages dependencies, risks, timelines, and blockers
- Communicates status upward (execs) and sideways (teams)
- Does NOT write code day-to-day

**Free study resources (2 hrs/day):**
- Atlassian: "What is a Technical Program Manager?" (atlassian.com)
- YouTube search: "TPM day in the life Google" / "TPM vs PM vs EM"
- Glassdoor: Read TPM interview reviews at Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Flipkart
- Read 5 TPM job descriptions daily - note recurring keywords

**Week 1–2 goal:** Write a 1-page document: "What a TPM does and why I'm a fit."

---

#### Week 3–4: Learn Core TPM Frameworks

**Agile / Scrum (mandatory):**
- Scrum.org: free resources + Scrum Guide PDF (the actual standard)
- Understand: sprints, backlog, standups, retrospectives, velocity, burndown
- Know the difference: Scrum vs Kanban vs SAFe

**Program Management fundamentals:**
- OKRs - how to write and track Objectives & Key Results
- RACI matrix - Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed
- RAID log - Risks, Assumptions, Issues, Dependencies
- Critical path method - which tasks block others
- Milestone planning vs task planning

**Tools (you already know Jira - go deeper):**
- Jira: epics, stories, subtasks, sprint boards, dashboards, filters
- Confluence: meeting notes, decision logs, runbooks
- Notion: docs, program wikis, meeting notes (widely used at Indian product startups - free personal plan)
- Miro/FigJam: dependency mapping (free tier)
- Google Sheets: program timeline / Gantt chart (use this heavily in interviews)

**Free courses:**
- Coursera: Google Project Management Certificate - audit free (no certificate)
- Atlassian University: Free Jira + Confluence training (university.atlassian.com)
- Scrum.org: Free learning path

## 4. Resume Strategy - Complete

### ATS Optimization (Critical - most resumes die here)

Companies use Applicant Tracking Systems that scan for keywords before a human ever sees your resume.

**ATS rules:**
- Use a clean, single-column format (no tables, columns, graphics)
- Use standard section names: "Work Experience", "Skills", "Education"
- Include exact keywords from job descriptions
- Submit as PDF (unless .docx is requested)
- No headers/footers with important info - ATS ignores them

**ATS keyword bank for TPM (use these throughout):**
```
Technical Program Manager, Program Management, Cross-functional coordination,
Stakeholder management, Agile, Scrum, Sprint planning, Backlog grooming,
Risk management, RAID log, Dependency tracking, Roadmap planning,
```

## 5A. MBA Leverage Strategy

Your MBA is more than a credential on your resume - it's an active tool most TPM candidates leave completely unused. Here's how to extract full value from it.

### Networking via MBA Network

**Batch-mates in PM/TPM/product roles:** Your highest-conversion referral pool. Send a message saying you're transitioning and ask for referrals or intros. People refer batch-mates more readily than LinkedIn strangers. Start with a LinkedIn search: `[Your MBA university] alumni → filter by "Technical Program Manager" or "Product Manager" → India`.

**Alumni at target companies:** Use your university's LinkedIn alumni tab. One warm alumni intro beats 30 cold outreach messages. This is especially powerful for Razorpay, Freshworks, PhonePe - companies that actively track MBA talent.

**University career services:** Many online MBA programs have placement partnerships with companies, or at minimum a job board. Check yours - some run hiring cycles specifically for MBA students.

### Portfolio Leverage

- **Frame MBA coursework as program delivery:** A group project is a multi-stakeholder program. An operations management assignment is a program planning exercise. Document your best ones like a TPM would and include them in your portfolio.
- **Use electives strategically:** If you have elective choices remaining, prioritize Operations Management, Project Management, and Technology Strategy - these directly feed your TPM narrative.
- **MBA capstone/thesis:** If you have one upcoming, align the topic to technology program management (e.g., "Agile adoption in Indian fintech startups" or "Program delivery frameworks for AI product teams"). This becomes a portfolio centerpiece.

### In Interviews

- **Weave MBA concepts naturally:** "One framework I've been building on from my MBA is..." - this demonstrates business thinking alongside execution capability.
- **Use MBA as proof of career intent:** "I'm not randomly pivoting - I'm building this transition deliberately: MBA for strategic thinking, TPM portfolio for delivery credibility, and 4.5 years of technical depth as the foundation." This directly neutralizes the "are you sure about this?" concern.
- **Finance + strategy literacy:** When asked about prioritization or tradeoffs, referencing ROI, NPV, or cost of delay correctly signals that you think like a business leader - rare in engineering-to-TPM candidates.

### Long-Term: MBA + TPM = TPdM Track

The MBA + engineering + TPM combination is exactly the profile companies recruit for at the Senior TPdM and Director level. Position this as your long-term differentiation from Month 1, not just when you're interviewing for TPdM roles at Month 18.

## 7. Interview Process - How It Works

### Typical TPM Interview Pipeline

```
Stage 1: Recruiter Screening Call (30 min)
   ↓
Stage 2: Hiring Manager Intro Call (45 min)
   ↓
Stage 3: Technical Round (45–60 min)
   ↓
Stage 4: Program Management Round (60 min)
   ↓
Stage 5: Behavioral / Leadership Round (45 min)
   ↓
Stage 6: Case Study / Design Round (60 min) - some companies
   ↓
Stage 7: Bar Raiser / Senior Leader (45 min) - Google/Amazon
   ↓
Offer
```

Not all companies do all stages. Mid-sized companies may compress to 3–4 rounds.

---

### Stage-by-Stage Breakdown

#### Stage 1: Recruiter Screening
**What they're checking:**
- Basic fit (title, experience, location, salary expectations)
- Communication quality
- Why TPM, why this company

**How to prepare:**
- Have your 90-second "story" ready (SWE → TPM transition narrative)
- Know the company's products / recent news
- Have salary range ready: "I'm targeting ₹18–25 LPA for the right role"
- Ask: "What does success look like in the first 90 days of this role?"

---

#### Stage 2: Hiring Manager Intro
**What they're checking:**
- Cultural fit
- Your motivation and self-awareness
- Whether you can think and communicate clearly

**How to prepare:**
- Research the hiring manager on LinkedIn (their background, what they post)
- Prepare 3 questions to ask them (show you've thought about the role deeply)
- Have 2–3 strong stories about coordination/leadership ready

**Questions to ask the HM:**
```
1. "What are the 2–3 biggest challenges the TPM team is facing right now?"
2. "What does a successful first 90 days look like for this role?"
3. "What's the team dynamic between TPM, PM, and engineering here?"
```

---

#### Stage 3: Technical Round
**What they're checking:**
- Do you understand how software is built?
- Can you speak engineer-to-engineer?
- Can you identify technical risks and dependencies?

**You won't be asked to code.** But you will be asked:
- "How would you identify technical risks in a program?"
- "An engineer says a feature will take 3 weeks. How do you validate that estimate?"
- "What's the difference between a microservices and monolith architecture?" (high-level)
- "How do you handle a situation where a team's technical debt is blocking your program?"

**How to prepare:**
- Be able to explain what an API is, what a database is, what CI/CD is - at the conceptual level
- Know common engineering failure modes: scope creep, tech debt, unclear requirements, integration issues
- Frame your ReactJS/Python experience as "I've built software - I understand the engineering reality"

---

#### Stage 4: Program Management Round
**What they're checking:**
- Can you manage a complex, multi-team program?
- Do you know TPM frameworks (RAID, dependencies, escalation paths)?
- Can you handle ambiguity?

**Common questions:**
- "Walk me through how you'd plan and execute a program with 4 teams and a 6-month timeline"
- "How do you manage a stakeholder who keeps changing scope?"
- "Two teams have conflicting priorities. How do you resolve it?"
- "You're 3 weeks from launch and a critical dependency is slipping. What do you do?"
- "How do you keep executives informed without overwhelming them?"

**Framework to answer program management questions:**
```
1. Clarify: "Can I ask a few questions to understand the scope?"
2. Structure: "Here's how I'd approach this..."
3. Risks: "The key risks I'd watch for are..."
4. Outcome: "Success looks like..."
```

**Prioritization frameworks - know these cold:**

| Framework | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **RICE** | Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort = score | Feature/backlog prioritization conversations |
| **MoSCoW** | Must-have, Should-have, Could-have, Won't-have | Scope negotiation with stakeholders |
| **WSJF** | Weighted Shortest Job First = Cost of Delay ÷ Duration | SAFe/Agile program sequencing |
| **Impact-Effort Matrix** | 2×2: high/low impact vs. high/low effort | Quick priority calls in a meeting |

You don't need to be an expert, but saying *"I'd use WSJF to sequence the work items by cost of delay"* signals senior TPM thinking. These come up both in Program Management rounds and Case Study rounds.

---

#### Stage 5: Behavioral / Leadership Round
**What they're checking:**
- How do you handle conflict, failure, ambiguity?
- Are you a leader or just a coordinator?
- Do you have self-awareness?

**Format:** STAR stories (Situation → Task → Action → Result)

**Common questions:**
- "Tell me about a time you drove alignment across teams that disagreed"
- "Describe a time a project failed. What did you learn?"
- "Tell me about a time you influenced without authority"
- "Describe a situation where you had to push back on leadership"
- "Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news to stakeholders"

> See Section 9 for your personal STAR story bank.

---

#### Stage 6: Case Study / Program Design
**What they're checking:**
- Can you think through a program from scratch?
- Can you communicate your thinking clearly?
- Do you prioritize well under pressure?

**Sample case prompt:**
```
"We're launching a new payments feature across 5 teams in 3 months.
You're the TPM. Walk us through how you'd manage this program."
```

**How to answer:**
```
Step 1 - Understand the program
"First, I'd kick off with a program charter - defining scope, success metrics,
and non-goals. I'd align all stakeholders on these before any work starts."

Step 2 - Map dependencies
"I'd run a dependency mapping workshop with all 5 teams - identify who needs
what from whom and in what order. Put this in a RAID log."

Step 3 - Build the timeline
"Working backward from the launch date, I'd build a milestone-based timeline with
buffer built in. No team commits to a date without engineering sign-off."

Step 4 - Communication plan
"Weekly status email to execs (RAG - Red/Amber/Green status). Daily standups
with team leads for 15 min. Slack channel for async blockers."

Step 5 - Risk management
"Top risks: scope creep, team A's dependency on team C, and unknown unknowns
in the payments infrastructure. Mitigations: scope freeze after Week 1,
daily sync with team A+C, 2-week buffer before launch."

Step 6 - Launch
"Code freeze 1 week before launch. Rollback plan defined and tested.
Post-launch monitoring for 48 hours."
```

---

#### Stage 7: Bar Raiser (Amazon) / Googleyness Round
**Amazon:** Focuses entirely on Leadership Principles. Have a STAR story for every LP.
**Google:** "Googleyness" - are you collaborative, curious, comfortable with ambiguity?

**Amazon Leadership Principles most relevant for TPM:**
- Deliver Results
- Earn Trust
- Dive Deep
- Bias for Action
- Think Big
- Invent and Simplify
- Are Right, A Lot

### Evaluating ESOPs and Stock Options - India Context

Indian startups frequently offer ESOPs as part of compensation. Here's how to evaluate them without getting burned.

| Factor | What to Ask | Red Flag |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Vesting schedule | "What's the cliff and vesting schedule?" | 4-year cliff, no gradual vesting |
| Strike price | "What is the strike price vs. current valuation?" | Strike price close to or above current FMV |
| Liquidity | "Has the company done secondary sales? Is there a buyback program?" | "We'll figure it out at IPO" with no timeline |
| ESOP pool size | "What % of total equity does the ESOP pool represent?" | <5% pool = your options are heavily diluted |
| Tax treatment | ESOPs taxed as perquisite at exercise + capital gains at sale | Understand your tax hit *before* accepting |

**Practical guidance:**
- At Series B/C startups: ESOPs can be meaningful - calculate % ownership, not just unit count
- At pre-Series A: Treat ESOPs as a lottery ticket, not real comp. Negotiate base independently.
- Always value ESOPs at ₹0 when negotiating base. Don't let a company justify a low base with "but you'll have ESOPs."
- Ask for the option grant letter with vesting schedule in writing before signing.

**Standard ask:** *"Could you share the ESOP grant document so I can review the vesting terms before giving you my final answer?"*

## 13. [Optional Path] TPdM Transition (Months 13–24)

> ⚠️ **This section is an ALTERNATIVE path - not your primary track.**
>
> Your Career Snap defines the **primary trajectory** as: Senior TPM → Principal TPM → Group TPM → Delivery Manager → Director of Programs/Delivery.
>
> TPdM (Technical Product Manager) is a lateral move into product strategy ownership. It diverges from the program delivery track. Only pursue this if you decide at Month 12+ that you want to own **what gets built** rather than **how and when it gets built**.
>
> If you stay on the TPM-native track, skip this section and focus on Section 12 (Month 10–12 promotion case building).

---

### What is TPdM?

A Technical Product Manager bridges the gap between TPM (delivery) and PM (strategy/vision).

| TPM | TPdM |
|-----|------|
| "How do we build this?" | "What should we build?" |
| Delivery-focused | Outcome-focused |
| Coordinates teams | Defines product direction |
| Manages timelines | Manages roadmaps |
| Reports to Eng/Program | Reports to Product/CPO |

---

### Skills to Build for TPdM

**Month 13–18 (while a TPM):**
- User research fundamentals: how to run user interviews, synthesize findings
- Product metrics: DAU, retention, NPS, conversion - what they mean and how to use them
- Writing PRDs (Product Requirements Documents) - practice with hypothetical features
- Market analysis: how to assess competitive landscape
- OKR writing: practice writing good objectives and measurable key results

**Free resources:**
- "Inspired" by Marty Cagan - find via Google Books preview or library
- Lenny's Newsletter (lennysnewsletter.com) - excellent free PM/TPdM content
- "Product School" YouTube channel - free PM fundamentals

**Month 18–24:**
- Build a TPdM portfolio: write 2–3 PRDs for real or hypothetical features
- Get involved in product discovery at your TPM job (ask the PM to include you)
- Lead a program where you also own the "why" not just the "how"

### Internal Switch vs. External Job Search for TPdM

This is a decision point most TPM-to-TPdM guides skip. The path you take depends on your company.

**Option A: Internal switch (preferred if your company has a strong product org)**

This is the lower-risk, higher-speed path. You already have credibility, context, and relationships - the hardest parts of a job search are done.

- Start embedding with the PM team from Month 13 - attend product reviews, offer to write PRD drafts, volunteer for user research synthesis.
- Have a direct, explicit conversation with your manager by Month 18: *"I want to grow toward TPdM. What would that look like here, and what's the timeline?"* Don't assume they'll offer it.
- Ask to co-own a feature's "what" alongside the "how" - propose it as a stretch project, not a role change.
- Risk: Some Indian companies have no TPdM role (only PM and TPM as separate tracks with no bridge). If that's your company → go to Option B.

**Option B: External job search for TPdM**

- Start quietly networking at Month 15–16 while still delivering as TPM. Don't wait until Month 24.
- Your new pitch: *"TPM with [X months] of program delivery + engineering background + MBA - ready to own product outcomes, not just delivery timelines."*
- Best-fit companies: Razorpay, PhonePe, Meesho, Flipkart (all have explicit TPdM tracks). Also: high-growth Series B/C startups with product-led growth models where one person can own delivery + direction.
- Target companies where your fintech domain knowledge is an advantage: Zerodha, Groww, Juspay, Setu. You can pitch yourself as "fintech TPdM" - an even narrower target with even less competition.

### 18.2 RAID Log

> **What it is:** A single living document tracking Risks, Assumptions, Issues, and Dependencies. Your most important ongoing artifact - it's your early warning system. Updated at every weekly sync. Anything unresolved after 2 weeks with no action = escalation candidate.

| ID    | Type | Description                        | Owner        | Status      | Impact (H/M/L) | Action / Mitigation              | Due Date   | Last Updated |
|-------|------|------------------------------------|--------------|-------------|----------------|----------------------------------|------------|--------------|
| R-001 | Risk | [Describe the risk]                | [Name/Role]  | Open        | H/M/L          | [What you're doing about it]     | YYYY-MM-DD | YYYY-MM-DD   |
| R-002 | Risk | [Describe the risk]                | [Name/Role]  | Monitoring  | H/M/L          | [What you're doing about it]     | YYYY-MM-DD | YYYY-MM-DD   |
| A-001 | Assm | [Assumption being made]            | [Name/Role]  | Unvalidated | H/M/L          | [How/when this will be validated]| YYYY-MM-DD | YYYY-MM-DD   |
| A-002 | Assm | [Assumption being made]            | [Name/Role]  | Validated   | M              | [Confirmed by whom]              | YYYY-MM-DD | YYYY-MM-DD   |
| I-001 | Issue| [Describe the active issue]        | [Name/Role]  | In Progress | H/M/L          | [Who is working on it, how]      | YYYY-MM-DD | YYYY-MM-DD   |
| I-002 | Issue| [Describe the active issue]        | [Name/Role]  | Escalated   | H              | [Escalated to whom, when]        | YYYY-MM-DD | YYYY-MM-DD   |

### 18.5 Risk Register

> **What it is:** A dedicated register for risks with probability × impact scoring. More detailed than RAID log risk rows. Reviewed monthly, or whenever program status changes to AMBER or RED.

| Risk ID | Risk Description            | Category   | Probability | Impact | Score (P×I) | Response           | Mitigation Plan          | Owner   | Status     | Last Reviewed |
|---------|-----------------------------|------------|-------------|--------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------------|---------|------------|---------------|
| RSK-001 | [What could go wrong]       | Technical  | H(3)        | H(3)   | 9           | Escalate           | Flagged to [Name]        | [Name]  | Escalated  | YYYY-MM-DD    |
| RSK-002 | [What could go wrong]       | Dependency | M(2)        | H(3)   | 6           | Mitigate           | [Action plan]            | [Name]  | Monitoring | YYYY-MM-DD    |
| RSK-003 | [What could go wrong]       | Resource   | L(1)        | M(2)   | 2           | Accept             | No action - acceptable   | [Name]  | Accepted   | YYYY-MM-DD    |
| RSK-004 | [What could go wrong]       | Scope      | H(3)        | M(2)   | 6           | Avoid              | Changed plan to eliminate| [Name]  | Closed     | YYYY-MM-DD    |

**Score guide:** 7–9 = 🔴 Immediate escalation | 4–6 = 🟡 Active mitigation + weekly monitoring | 1–3 = 🟢 Accept or watch passively

**Response types:** `Mitigate` (reduce P or I) | `Accept` (live with it) | `Transfer` (shift to vendor/other team) | `Avoid` (change the plan) | `Escalate` (above your authority)
